Yaganti Uma Maheswara Temple

The Yaganti Uma Maheswara Temple, situated 14 km from Banaganipalli in Kurnool district, Andhra Pradesh, is a renowned Hindu shrine dedicated to Lord Shiva, Parvati, and Nandi. Constructed in the 15th century by King Harihara Bukka Raya of the Sangama Dynasty of the Vijayanagara Empire, this temple follows Vaishnavaite traditions and stands as a testament to the architectural prowess of its time

The temple complex is renowned for its unique origins and intriguing legends. According to local lore, Sage Agastya initially intended to build a temple for Lord Venkateswara on this site. However, due to a mishap with the idol, the sage performed a penance to Shiva, who then declared the site more suitable for him, resembling Kailash. Shiva then granted a request for a Parvathi Goddess idol in a single stone, known as Lord Uma Maheswara.

Another fascinating legend involves Chitteppa, a devotee who, while worshipping Shiva, witnessed the deity in the form of a tiger. Overwhelmed with joy, Chitteppa danced and shouted "Neganti Shivanu ne kanti" ("I saw Shiva, I saw"), leading to the creation of a nearby cave named after him.

The temple also holds significance for its annual Maha Shivaratri celebrations, which attract numerous devotees from across Andhra Pradesh. The saint Lord Veerabrahmendra Swami, who resided here for a time, authored the famous text, Kalagnanam.

Interesting Facts

  • Construction and Tradition: Built in the 15th century by King Harihara Bukka Raya of the Vijayanagara Empire, the temple is constructed according to Vaishnavaite traditions, highlighting its historical and cultural significance.
  • Legend of Sage Agastya:The original intent to build a temple for Lord Venkateswara led to a divine intervention by Shiva, who preferred the site for himself, thus resulting in the consecration of the Uma Maheswara idol.
  • Chitteppa's Vision:The temple is linked to the legend of Chitteppa, a devotee who saw Shiva in the form of a tiger, leading to the creation of a cave named after him.
  • Nandi Murti Phenomenon:The Nandi murti (bull statue) in front of the temple is believed to be continuously increasing in size. Locals claim that it was initially much smaller and has grown over time due to the expansion of the mineral-rich rock from which it is carved. The temple staff have had to remove a pillar to accommodate the increasing size of the Nandi.
